Public support for police body-worn cameras has grown in the wake of several high-profile shooting deaths. Law enforcement agencies across the country have invested in cameras to improve accountability and transparency, but how and when they’re are used and whether the footage gets released is still up for debate.

“A lot hangs in those details,” said Nancy La Vigne, director of Urban Institute’s Justice Policy Center. “Those policies and practices can make the technology more or less powerful in terms of their intended impact. And there’s a high degree of variation among states.”

In Chicago, video of an officer fatally shooting 17-year-old Laquan McDonald wasn’t released for 400 days. While the footage in that case came from dash-cams and not body-worn cameras, questions about when—or whether—to release the videos touch on the same issues. A new mayoral task force charged with reforming police accountability in response to McDonald’s death will also review the city’s video-release policy.

But Chicago is not alone in grappling with the controversial policies surrounding police cameras. Several cities are deploying this new technology even as legislation governing its use is being written, which can lead to confusion when department policies bump up against wiretapping and public records laws already on the books.

“In one jurisdiction, the state statutes around eavesdropping were inhibiting their ability to use the cameras the way they had envisioned,” La Vigne said. “You can imagine law enforcement wondering how useful these cameras are if they need to ask permission to record. How does that apply if you’re in a high-speed chase?”

Some state wiretap laws exempt police from securing consent under certain circumstances. Officers in California, for example, do not need to tell suspects that they’re being filmed.

In Pennsylvania, their wiretap law prevents police from recording inside a person’s home without permission, but state legislators are seeking to amend that law for police officers.

In the meantime, Pittsburgh police officers have been instructed to shut off their cameras if they get a call dispatching them to a house, said Lt. Ed Trapp, project manager for the department’s body-worn camera program. Erring on the side of caution ensures that officers won’t have to pause in an emergency situation to turn the camera off or ask for permission to record.

“We’re working on getting the legislation changed,” Trapp said. “Going into a house is one of the most obtrusive things government does….Why would that be the one time you’d want to turn the cameras off?”

In addition to questions about recording in a person’s home, existing laws leave a lot of gray areas about when officers can turn their cameras on and off. In practice, officers have considerable discretion over when to activate their cameras, La Vigne said, which means a lot of opportunities to miss out on crucial footage intentionally or not.

“There need to be very clear, well-defined policies about when the camera should and should not be on,” said Jay Stanley, senior policy analyst at the ACLU. Uncertainty could open the door to officers abusing their discretion, Stanley said, so clear policies are needed to protect the privacy of crime victims or potential informants.

Striking a balance between privacy and transparency

In supporting body-worn cameras, the public may assume that they will have access to the footage, La Vigne said, but that’s not always the case.

In early 2015, in response to public records requests, the Seattle Police Department launched their own YouTube channel of body-cam videos. But the redacted videos are so blurred out, it’s hard to make out what’s being filmed. In Pittsburgh, the police department’s current policy is not to release any footage, Trapp said, but the department is waiting to see if the legislature will address how public records requests specifically apply to police body-camera videos.

“There are certain states whose public records requests are very broad and basically make all the video releasable, and we think that could be a real privacy problem,” Stanley said. “But there are other states and departments where the police are trying to release nothing, and that is also not the right balance.”

Kerry Condon, president of the Anaheim Police Association, said that officers in Anaheim treat body-worn cameras as an evidence collection tool, and the footage, therefore, as evidence that should not be released in an ongoing investigation. Exceptions for high-profile cases would need to be considered on a case-by-case basis by the chief of police.

Condon helped develop the body-worn camera policy used by Anaheim police. “It’s one of the most important policies that I’ve dealt with in 25 years of law enforcement,” he said. “Right now, until a state policy comes into effect, every city or municipality has free rein on how they want to create their own policy.”

While support for body-worn cameras is high, La Vigne cautions that deployment may be speeding ahead of policy development and evaluation. Policy details about how cameras and videos are used, implemented, shared, and stored matter—and the legal questions are still being worked out state by state.

“This is a very new technology and everyone is trying to figure it out,” Stanley said. “We’re hopeful that over time, we’ll see a national movement toward a set of best practices…that are widely accepted by police departments.”
